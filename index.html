Part 1 Overview of progress

1.1 Overview of regional progress

With just five years remaining to achieve the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, the Asia-Pacific region stands at a critical crossroads. Its economic dynamism, demographic weight and innovation potential position it as a global leader. Yet this promise is overshadowed by persistent challenges, including rapid urbanization, climate vulnerability, widening inequalities and demographic shifts. At the same time, transformative technologies, in particular artificial intelligence and digital innovations, present a double-edged sword: offering powerful tools for inclusive growth while risking the deepening of existing divides. The decisions made today will determine whether the region harnesses its strengths to secure a sustainable future for all or falls short of its commitments.

The analysis reveals a stark contradiction: hard-won gains in eradicating poverty, building infrastructure and improving health outcomes are being eroded by environmental decline and stagnation in progress towards goals designed to ensure no one is left behind. Current data leave no doubt that achieving the SDGs will require urgent and fundamental shifts in policy and resource allocation. The shifts will need to be coherent and should consider how improving one Goal may affect another Goal.

The data tell two contrasting stories: one where the region is advancing and another where it is falling behind. The region continues to make notable progress towards SDG 9 (Industry, innovation and infrastructure), which has no targets in decline, and SDG 3 (Good health and well-being), bolstered by steady reductions in maternal and child mortality. There have been improvements towards SDG 1 (No poverty) and SDG 6 (Clean water and sanitation), though these areas still require acceleration. However, despite these achievements, the region is not on track to achieve any of the 17 SDGs, underscoring the urgent need for coordinated and accelerated action.

Advances achieved so far are now at risk. An alarming pattern of regression is undermining previous gains, most notably in the environmental sphere. SDG 14 (Life below water) faces significant setbacks in sustainable fishing and coastal conservation. Progress on SDG 13 (Climate action) remains a grave concern as the level of total greenhouse gas emissions in the region continues to increase. Similarly, progress towards SDG 15 (Life on land) is hampered by accelerating biodiversity loss and land degradation.

On the current trajectory, the region will miss 103 of the 117 measurable targets. The lack of adequate data for 52 targets underscores a critical evidence gap, limiting the ability to track progress and inform effective implementation. Existing data show that the region is on track to achieve only 14 targets by 2030, with seven SDGs having no targets on track.

Evidence also warns that the pledge to “leave no one behind” is faltering, as seen in setbacks on target 4.5 (on ensuring equal access to education) and target 16.3 (on ensuring equal access to justice). Furthermore, insufficient data on SDG 5 (Gender equality) and SDG 16 (Peace, justice, and strong institutions), goals central to this ambition, obscure our understanding of how effectively the most vulnerable are being reached.

 

1.2 SDG profiles by indicators

SDG 1: No Poverty

Progress in reducing income poverty is a key achievement for the region. However, these gains are increasingly at risk due to setbacks in limiting the human and economic toll of natural disasters. Data highlight a critical and unmet need to strengthen disaster resilience and preparedness. At the same time, the decline in official development assistance (ODA) grants for poverty reduction in least developed countries (LDCs) remains a serious concern, threatening support for the most vulnerable. Prioritizing government spending on essential services is therefore vital to building inclusive and resilient societies.

SDG 2: Zero hunger

The region is making slow progress in combating malnutrition, although it has been faster in reducing undernourishment and good progress is being made on long-term food security. However, this progress is at risk due to setbacks in indicators related to sustainable agricultural practices and the preservation of local breeds. This trend underscores the urgent need to scale up investment in the agriculture sector and prioritize sustainable farming practices across the region.

SDG 3: Good health and well-being

The region continues to show strong progress in health and well-being, with sustained reductions in maternal, neonatal and under-5 mortality. Adolescent birth rates are declining and ODA for medical research and basic health sectors has substantially increased over the past decade. However, this positive trajectory faces persistent challenges: high risks associated with noncommunicable diseases, suicide and use of tobacco and alcohol; rising bloodstream infections caused by antimicrobial-resistant organisms; high household health expenditures; and low health worker density. Addressing these issues is critical to safeguarding the region’s hard-won health gains and building resilient health systems for all.

SDG 4: Quality education

The region has maintained steady progress on foundational education, with consistent school completion rates, growing pre-primary enrolment and improved access to basic services. However, a critical gap remains – learning outcomes continue to fall short, with regression in achieving minimum proficiency in reading and mathematics. This highlights the urgent need to move beyond access and focus on quality of education, through strengthened teacher training and targeted efforts to address inequalities, so that all students acquire the essential skills they need.

SDG 5: Gender equality

Progress towards gender equality in the region remains difficult to assess due to significant data gaps. Where data are available, indicators on women's representation in managerial positions and political positions show only slow improvement. This highlights an urgent need to invest in robust gender statistics, which are essential for tracking progress and ultimately achieving gender equality.

SDG 6: Clean water and sanitation

The region continues to make slow but steady progress in expanding access to essential water and sanitation services, including safely managed drinking water and reductions in open defecation. However, these infrastructural gains are being severely undermined by a continued regression in the extent of permanent water bodies, an indicator of the health of freshwater ecosystems. This persistent environmental decline, coupled with high water stress, threatens the long-term sustainability and availability of water resources for future generations.

SDG 7: Affordable and clean energy

The region continues to make strong progress in expanding access to electricity and is set to reach the 2030 target ahead of schedule. However, the crucial transition to renewable energy has stalled. The share of renewables in the total energy mix is declining, revealing a significant disconnect between energy access goals and the imperative for a clean energy transition. Accelerating investment in renewable energy capacity is essential to align the region’s energy sector with climate and sustainability objectives.

SDG 8: Decent work and economic growth

The region has continued to experience economic growth, with progress on Aid for Trade in LDCs and financial inclusion. However, the current growth model remains environmentally unsustainable. Indicators on material footprint and domestic material consumption show slow or stagnant progress, signalling a persistent challenge in decoupling economic activity from resource use and environmental degradation. At the same time, progress on reducing informal employment and advancing youth employment strategies remains limited, underscoring the ongoing challenge of ensuring that economic gains translate into decent work for all. The most concerning trends under SDG 8 are compliance with labour rights and occupational injuries, both areas where the region is regressing. 

SDG 9: Industry, innovation and infrastructure

The region continues to demonstrate strong performance in industry, innovation and infrastructure, supported by near-universal mobile network coverage. Progress in manufacturing value added has been slow and is not fully balanced, as progress in manufacturing employment has begun to regress. Furthermore, greening the industrial sector remains a significant challenge. While carbon dioxide emissions from manufacturing are improving and are almost on track, additional support for small-scale industries and medium- and high-tech industries is essential to accelerate progress on these indicators.

SDG 10: Reduced inequalities

Progress in reducing inequalities remains slow and insufficient. Key indicators of income distribution, such as the Gini index and the share of the population living on less than half the median income, show little improvement. The continued decline in the share of labour income in GDP coupled with the growing human cost of migration underscores the deep-rooted and multifaceted nature of inequality in the region.

SDG 11: Sustainable cities and communities

The region continues to make progress in developing national strategies for disaster risk reduction and improving urban air quality. However, cities and communities remain highly vulnerable, as indicators measuring the human and economic toll from disasters show a worrying decline. This persistent pattern, including damage to critical infrastructure, underscores a dangerous gap between planning and real-world resilience, threatening the sustainability of urban development.

SDG 12: Responsible consumption and production

The region continues to make slow progress in areas such as corporate sustainability reporting, hazardous waste disposal and electronic waste recycling. In addition, the fundamental shift towards sustainable production and consumption remains a significant challenge. Indicators on material footprint and domestic material consumption are not on track, signalling a persistent failure to decouple economic growth from resource use and environmental degradation. This deeply unsustainable trend is further entrenched by the continued regression on ending fossil-fuel subsidies, which actively undermines progress towards a green economy.

SDG 13: Climate action

The region continues to make progress in developing national and local strategies for disaster risk reduction. However, this is overshadowed by a critical failure in implementing meaningful climate action. Greenhouse gas emissions continue to rise, and the human impact of climate-related disasters remains severe. These challenges are compounded by significant data gaps for key climate indicators, limiting the ability to fully assess the crisis and the region’s capacity to respond effectively. This combination of regressing trends and information gaps underscores a dangerous deficit in tangible outcomes needed to secure a safe and sustainable future.

SDG 14: Life below water

The health of the region’s marine ecosystems is in serious decline. The economic contribution of sustainable fisheries continues to decline, reflecting mounting pressure on marine resources. While some gains have been made in establishing protected marine areas, these efforts remain insufficient to counter the broader trend of deterioration. Compounding the challenge is a reduction in reported indicators, limiting a comprehensive assessment of marine health. Nevertheless, the available data clearly signal an ongoing crisis for life below water.

SDG 15: Life on land

The region’s terrestrial biodiversity remains under severe threat. The Red List Index, a critical indicator of species extinction risk, continues to show a downward trend, indicating an accelerating loss of biodiversity. This decline is compounded by setbacks in addressing land degradation. While there has been limited progress in designating protected areas for terrestrial and mountain biodiversity and promoting sustainable forest management, these efforts are far from sufficient to halt the overall decline. The continued negative trends highlight an urgent need for more effective conservation measures and large-scale land restoration.

SDG 16: Peace, justice and strong institutions

Progress towards peaceful and inclusive societies remains difficult to assess due to persistent data gaps for critical indicators, including corruption and trafficking in persons. Among the available metrics, the region shows only slow progress in areas such as government efficiency and the representation of women and youth in parliament. However, this is offset by regression in reducing the number of unsentenced detainees, highlighting significant challenges within justice systems. The limited evidence base itself underscores the profound difficulty of building effective and accountable institutions.

SDG 17: Partnerships for the Goals

The region’s progress on building partnerships essential for achieving the SDGs remains uneven. While indicators measuring digital connectivity and international trade show strength, systemic challenges continue to undermine efforts. Progress is regressing on reducing debt service burdens, diverting crucial resources away from sustainable development. Furthermore, efforts to strengthen national statistical capacities remain slow, perpetuating data gaps that hinder effective monitoring and implementation of the 2030 Agenda.

Part 2 Data availability

2.1 Data availability in the Asia-Pacific region

The Asia-Pacific region continues to make progress in producing data for the SDG indicators, though advancement remains gradual. Data availability shows an upward trend, with an average of 55 per cent of indicators having at least two data points in 2025. However, significant gaps persist, as a substantial share of indicators still lack sufficient data.

Analysis across the SDGs reveals persistent disparities. Large data gaps remain for SDG 5 (Gender equality) and SDG 16 (Peace, justice and strong institutions), while SDG 14 (Life below water), SDG 13 (Climate action) and SDG 11 (Sustainable cities and communities) also show lower data availability compared to other Goals.

Countries and territories have improved SDG data availability through stronger coordination within national statistical systems and through improved collaboration with international agencies.

Strengthening national statistical systems requires not only generating more data but also leveraging existing data effectively to inform policy and accelerate SDG progress. Achieving the data transformation needed for the 2030 Agenda demands sustained political and financial investment, coupled with robust coordination and partnerships across the region.

Granular data are essential for understanding differences among specific populations in achieving the SDGs and identifying those at risk of falling behind.

An assessment of data availability in the Asia-Pacific region shows that granular data is most common by age, sex and migratory status. Among the 38 indicators that can be disaggregated by age, economies in the region have, on average, two or more data points for 33 per cent of them, which is higher than for any other dimension. Data by sex is available for the largest number of indicators (57), though only 27 per cent have sufficient data.

Annex 1: Technical notes - methodology to measure progress

The Asia-Pacific SDG progress assessment is based on the global indicator framework for the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and targets of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development as adopted by the General Assembly on 6 July 2017 and further refined in subsequent years. Data for this analysis are primarily sourced from the Global SDG Indicators Database maintained by the Statistics Division of the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs. When sufficient data on a defined SDG indicator are not available, additional indicators from internationally recognized sources are used. All indicators are available on the ESCAP SDG Gateway Data Explorer along with the information on country and territory groupings and definitions.  To avoid bias towards larger economies, regional and subregional averages are calculated using simple averages rather than weighted aggregates. This section outlines the methodology used for SDG progress assessment. More resources are available on the Asia-Pacific SDG Gateway.

Selection of indicators

Indicators are included based on two criteria:

  • Data availability: two or more data points for more than 50 per cent of the countries and territories in the relevant region or group;
  • Target setting: ability to define a quantitative target value.

If an indicator fails to meet either criteria, it is excluded from the analysis. The full list of indicators with their respective target values is contained in annex 2annex 2 of this report.

Measures for tracking progress

Two principal measures are used to assess progress towards the SDGs: the current status index and the anticipated progress index. The indices answer two different questions:

  1. Current status index: how much progress has been made since 2015?
  2. Anticipated progress index: how likely is it that the target will be achieved by 2030?

The anticipated progress index measures the gap between the predicted indicator value and the target. Both indices are constructed at the sub-indicator (a sub-indicator being a series, disaggregation, or subcomponent of an indicator) and can be aggregated at the indicator, target or Goal level.

In this report, the current status index is presented at the Goal level and the anticipated progress index is presented at the target. Both indices are presented at the indicator level on the Asia-Pacific SDG Gateway. While the current status index aims to provide a robust measure across all 17 SDGs, limited data availability for some Goals and sensitivity to the addition of new indicators mean results should be interpreted with caution. Given the significant increase in indicators and data availability since previous editions, comparisons with earlier reports are not recommended.

For any indicator where the current value has already met or exceeded the target, the current status index is not calculated and is assigned a score of 10.

Anticipated progress index

This index measures anticipated progress with the targeted goal. It does so by projecting the indicator value for 2030 and benchmarking this predicted value against the target. The index provides a measure of how much additional progress will still be required by the end of 2030 to reach the target, assuming the current pace of change is sustained. Denoting the predicted value of an indicator for 2030 by It, the anticipated progress index can be computed by replacing Icv with It in the formulas for the current status index.

 

 

The anticipated progress index is only calculated for indicators that are not expected to achieve the target. When the predicted value has already met or exceeded the target, or when it is expected to reach the target by 2030, the indicator is automatically classified as “will be achieved”.

Aggregation

A total of 170 indicators were used to compute the current status index for the 2025 SDG progress assessment. However, due to insufficient data, 12 indicators could not be projected for 2030 and were therefore excluded from the anticipated progress index calculations. When multiple variations of an indicator exist (for example health worker density), all variants are used in calculations. Each variant is assigned a weight such that the sum of the weights under the indicator is 1. A weighted average is then calculated to produce a single index value for that indicator.

Disaggregated statistics

Disaggregation by sex, location or a combination of age and sex is available for 27 indicators. To incorporate disaggregated statistics, a vulnerable group is identified for each group as the subgroup that is the worst off among all subpopulation groups. For each series, progress is then measured as the average of the progress achieved by the vulnerable group and the reference population. This approach ensures that progress on each series reflects improvements made in the most vulnerable group, thereby adjusting overall progress for equity considerations.

Extrapolation methods

Producing the two measures of progress requires predicting and imputing missing values for both the current year and previous years. These estimates are generated using a weighted regression model that applies time-related weights, based on the assumption that the importance attached to the indicator values should be proportional to how recent the data are.

Suppose n data points are available for indicator I in a given region over a period of T years, and we aim to estimate its value for the year t.

T = tn – t1 where tn is the latest year and t1 is the earliest year, for which data on indicator I are available. The time-related weights work as multipliers that adjust the rate of change in each period in proportion to its temporal distance to the target year (t). Setting regional target values

Of the 169 SDG targets, only 37 per cent have explicit or implicit target values. For the rest, target values have been set using a “champion area” approach, which leverages historical feasibility and optimizes the use of available data.

The approach works as follows:

  1. Identify the top performing countries in the region for a given indicator;
  2. Calculate their average rate of change and use this as the target rate for the region.

Conceptually, if all the top performers for a specific indicator were grouped into a single hypothetical area, the “champion area”, its rate of change would represent the benchmark for the region. If the region as a whole can match this rate from 2015 to 2030, the target value should be achieved.

The regional target value is then derived by applying the champion area’s rate of change to the regional baseline (average indicator value across all countries and territories with available data). In cases where the champion area approach cannot be applied, the average value of the top five performers (based on the latest available data) is used as the regional target.

Trend assessment (section 1.3)

At the national level, the assessment in this report focuses on analysing the trends in the absence of widely adopted target values by countries. For a given indicator, the direction of change over time toward or away from the desirable direction determines whether it is classified as a positive or negative trend. Likewise, when the change is less than 0.5 per cent, then an indicator is classified as stagnant. To aggregate the score for each SDG Target, the following scoring is used for averaging the performance at the indicator level:

  • 1 for positive trends,
  • -1 for negative trends,
  • 0 for stagnant trends.

The trends in SDG Targets are classified as positive or negative based on thresholds of 0.25 or –0.25, respectively. Otherwise, a stagnation is assumed.
